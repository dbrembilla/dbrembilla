Ontology

Travel Relational View. A travel centred ontology.

Knowledge Representation and Extraction exam.

Project description

The objective of this project is the definition of an ontology about travels, especially envisioned for history and historians but not limited to it.
To define the ontology, first the core concepts of the ontology was defined; then the eXtreme Design (XD) process was followed (with the exception of the customer involvement part). This means that the design will be requirements-driven, test-driven and iterative. It will start from a series of stories, from which Competency Questions (CQs) will follow; the answers to this can be either Contextual Statements (CSs), inserting new constraints or notions to the ontology, or Reasoning Requirements (RRs), which one can get from inferences from known elements. This methodology also employs Ontology Design Patterns (ODPs), that are specialised for the ontology in use.
This ontology aims at describing historical events, thus should allow for maximum flexibility.

Ontology Foundation

The centre of the ontology are the event, agent and context classes (these concepts were similarly defined in the ArCo ontology for cultural events and in the Nary participation ODP).

trv:event
Central to the ontology. It represents a generic event in history. All events have at least one contextual information in which they happen.
trv:agent
Describes an active agent that acts within an event. All agents have an event within which they act.
trv:context
describes in a generic way the context in which an event happens.
trv:hasContext
connects an event with the context in which it happens.
trv:isInvolvedWith
Defines the involvement of an agent with an event.

Story-led Design

First Story

Having described these core elements of the ontology, I proceeded to apply the XD methodology to further develop it.

The explorer John Cabot travelled by boat under the commission of Henry VII of England. He explored North America, landing in Newfoundland, in multiple travels, in particular during his first travel he started in May 1497 from Bristol, landed in today's Newfoundland on June 24 1497 and returned to Bristol in August 1497. This travel can be inserted in the historical context of XV-XVI century colonisation of America. His objective was to arrive to the Indies.

CQ Answer Classes and Properties
What is a travel? What constitutes it? A travel is an event. All known travels happen in an historical context. Travels can happen in different mediums, in particular it might be a maritime, air, land or space travels. trv:travel, subclass of trv:event and superclass of trv:maritimeTravel and of the other kinds of travel; trv:historicalContext. All travels must have a trv:hasHistoricalContext trv:historicalContext property. Another relevant event for a travel is trv:preparation, which is the preparation for another event; since the preparation for a travel could be important for the travel itself.
Who participates in the travel? What role can they have? Agents could be individuals or groups. All agents act in some event. Agents have a role, which is a distinct class for which individuals can be created to define new roles. Existing ODPs are the Objectrole and the ParticipationRole ODPs. trv:agent has trv:person and trv:groupOfPeople as subclasses to identify different cases for identifying different agents. Agents must have trv:isInvolvedWith trv:historicalContext property and trv:hasRole a trv:role; agents are different than their role. trv:role are trv:traveller, defining the role of a traveller, such as an explorer, or trv:preparationRole for other roles, such as commissioner, involved with the organization of the travel.
In which context an event happen? When did an event happen? Which places did the event took place in? Within the context, we can distinguish historical context, destination, time and places. Travels happen within a certain timespan, during which they might touch different places in different times. Subclasses of trv:context are trv:time, trv:place, trv:objective. All events have a trv:historicalContext, which has a trv:hasTimespan trv:timespan property. Travels can have a trv:destination, a supposed destination for the travel which might differ from the place of arrival (e.g. Asia would be the destination for this story). In order to represent place and time during an event, a blank node connected with trv:hasCoordinates is used.

Second Story

The Apollo 11 spaceflight expedition, part of the Apollo Space Project flights, led to the first Moon Landing. The expedition took flight by using the Saturn V rocket.

CQ Answer Classes and Properties
Is the event part of a bigger series of events? What is its objective? An event could be part of a bigger series of events. An ODP in this sense would be RecurrentEventSeries; I simplified this pattern for this ontology for more flexibility within this ontology. The objective of an event may or may not be explicit, but it can be informative to add to a model of an event. trv:isPartOf is a transitive property that can connect events with super-events and trv:relatesTo to connect events with relevant ones. trv:hasObjective trv:objective can be used to highlight the objective of an event.
Was a vehicle used in the event? An event, such as a travel, might use a vehicle. This can be useful as some vehicles became famous. trv:vehicle can be used in this case, along with its subclasses like trv:spaceVehicle.

Third Story

Marco Polo’s travels are reported in the book "Il Milione". Whether or not the travel happened is disputed.

CQ Answer Classes and Properties
What is the source of information about the travel? Travels can have a limited amount of sources, thus it can be useful to represent them. The trv:source class represents the source of a narration of an event, with a variety of subclasses to more accurately describe it. The trv:describes property and its subproperties describes where an event has its source.
Is the fact that an event happened disputed? A fact, such as that a travel happened, might be disputed by scholars. The property trv:isDisputed can be used to highlighy a disputed connection between elements or of an element itself.

Fourth Story

In this last story, I will look at Negative Competency Questions (NCQs), meaning questions that my ontology won't answer.
The Fellowship of the Ring’s travels are part of the fantasy book “The Lord of the Rings”, a piece of fiction. Their travels were inherently good, as they tried to defeat evil. The Fellowship split up multiple times throughout the journey.

NCQ Answer
Is the event just fiction? This ontology can be used for events narrated in fiction without being considered disputed.
Are the consequences of an event good or evil? Are the people in the event evil? This ontology will abstain from value judgements about events.
Is an agent involved in an event for its entire length? This ontology will consider agents who are involved in an event indipendently from the duration of their partecipation.

}
